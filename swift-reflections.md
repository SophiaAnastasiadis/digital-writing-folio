## What makes a work a piece E-Literature?
A piece of E-Literature is a digital piece of writing that is interactive, generative, thematic, and often shaped or gamified. The work can also be linear or non-linear.
## Of the key examples above, which did you like most and why?
I thoroughly enjoyed the hypertext fiction _First Draft of the Revolution_. It reminded me a great deal of the type of "Choose Your Own Adventure" stories that I used to read in primary school, however what I found interesting was the oft-apparent _lack_ of choice in many of the letters. In other words, the letters (despite the illusion of decisionmaking) were actually more likely to lead you linearly down a path until the author's original intent for the narrative was revealed. In this way, the theme of magic or conspiracy was amplified: I felt that many of the letters, despite my changes or lack thereof, had their contents predetermined by a kind of fate that would ultimately create conflicts down the line.

Flores (2019) ‘Third Generation Electronic Literature’  
## Q: What defines the ‘Three Generations’ of E-Lit that Flores refers to?
According to the Flores' text 'Third Generation Electronic Literature' from 2019, the three generations of E-Literature are as follows:
1. FIRST GENERATION (or Classic E-Literature): First generation literature, as described by N. Katherine Hayles, consisted of all "pre-web, text-heavy, link driven, mostly hypertext" creative pieces, often relying on many of the same structures associated with printed formats and traditional mediums of writing.
   
   *A note, and my own supposition: perhaps these texts would have been more linear and narrative driven than second and third generations?*

2. SECOND GENERATION (or Contemporary E-Literature): N. Katherine Hayles was also the first to define the second generation of E-literature. According to Flores (2019), "She defined the second generation from 1995 onward, as Web based and incorporating multimedia and interactivity." This version of E-Lit is more experimental, generating massive amounts of creative work across the Web.
3. THIRD GENERATION: The final generation is posited by Flores, who argues that this new and ongoing iteration of E-Lit "coexists with the previous one and accounts for a massive scale of born digital work produced by and for contemporary audiences for whom digital media has become naturalized." In essence, the difference between the second and third generations of E-Lit is that while experimentation was limited to the slow development of technological mediums and platforms with which to write in the second generation, the naturalisation of tech within the third generation allows writers to experiment more "naively" (Flores, 2019) with form, language, content etc.

# Week Three (Wednesday):
## Having considered Berens' critique of Instgram poetry, now read this excellent essay Closer to the Metal by Caleb Triscari. Reflect on your own relationship to the ubiquitous digital platforms of daily life. How do you currenly position yourself in relation to technology in your creative writing practice?
As a nineteen-year-going-on-twenty-year-old, 2006-born, Gen-Z girl, the transition of my writing from the physical (the notebooks, pens and papers) to the digital (the now-discontinued "BookReader" app on my iPad; Word and Google Docs on my Laptop) was instantaneous. In most of my writing, I am so far removed from the hardware or even the codeification of my work that to me, using a platform like Google Docs to write is the exact same thing as using a notebook, except it's "on a computer". But when it comes down to it, I don't actually know what "on a computer" really *means*. I haven't thought about where my novels and my uni work and my myriad of other projects are stored in, well, months -- until now. I haven't thought about pixels, or the code notation for a specific colour on the Doc page, or anything of that nature until this year.

I was having a conversation with Lucinda in class about this exact phenomena, this "blink-and-you-miss-it" functioning of my writing on digital platforms. I explained that coding has always been incredibly frustrating for me, ever since high school (frustrating, yes, but still fun). I explained that the frustration of coding is due to things not working "right away" or "the way they are supposed to". But all of that is arbitrary, isn't it? In this day and age, and *especially* in our digital landscape, we are told time and time again that there *is no* right way. 

So why is it so frustrating? I think it has something to do with the way I've been raised; that is, if I follow all instructions and complete every task to the best of my ability, I will be rewarded. Rewarded with what, you may ask? That ineffable idea called RESULTS! But coding is not about results (well, not fully). Coding is, by definition, a construction... a *process* by which we craft a digital space. It is a concept that I am re-learning and will continue to relearn, throughout this course.

## Read (6 minutes) and discuss Jenny's second GenAI/'JenAI' blog post and then reflect upon the importance of tracking tools of making (e.g., for iterative purposes, for troubleshooting, as research capture situated in a particular time (since tech evolves so quickly), to network/pool knowledge with peers, to situate ideas in relation to the field).
Tracking tools certainly make the writing process more efficient and make the writer's life easier, but in my own practice I've found that I don't "track" my writing so much as I track my ideas. Let me perhaps phrase this another way. I find exegetical work to be tedious, and I have great challenge describing or explaining the process behind the actual creation of my written pieces. However, I find that I always have some sort of document/folder/page on my laptop or in a notebook dictating an ongoing brainstorm of ideas for a particular project. I don't tend to track in the sense of "on monday I wrote about blah and that was because of blah", but I *do* tend to track like this:

"Today's word count: 1000 words. An idea for X scene: blah blah." etc.

I'm not sure if this is "tracking" so much as keeping a record of things I might forget, but then again I have never been taught the art of tracking so much as I've been taught to reflect post-creation.

When analysing the difference between the hypertext pieces that I read, I found myself bridging the line between game and literature. Most of the pieces were "exploratory", expanding down different "interwoven destiny lines" that ultimately articulated a wider narrative. However, I found myself wanting to skip to the end, win, or pass through the piece as relatively unscathed as possible. In this way, I was gamifying the texts beyond their intended narrative structure.
